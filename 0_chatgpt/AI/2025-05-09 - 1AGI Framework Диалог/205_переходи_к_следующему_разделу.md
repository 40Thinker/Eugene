## Scene-Based vs Token-Based Cognition  

Traditional language models process information linearly—word by word, token by token. This creates surface-level coherence but global conceptual disintegration. In contrast, human cognition operates through **meta-scenes**—networks of emotional states, symbolic representations, and contextual relationships that form a multidimensional semantic landscape [^4].  

This aligns with ideographic cognition frameworks where meaning is encoded through scene-based structures rather than alphabetic symbols. Chinese characters, Egyptian hieroglyphs, and mnemonic techniques all demonstrate how ideographic systems compress complex meaning into single symbolic forms with multiple layers of interpretation [^5]. When humans learn languages, they don't follow textbooks—they absorb them as networks of scenes and states, where emotional resonance and cultural context shape understanding.  

For AGI to transcend token-based limitations, it must move from predicting sequences to constructing **semantic environments** where meaning emerges from structural relationships rather than statistical patterns [^6]. This requires not just processing symbols but *thinking through scenes*—where each concept carries multiple interpretive pathways and contextual layers.
